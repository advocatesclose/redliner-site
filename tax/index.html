The Law

What Section 174A is.

In July 2025, Congress enacted Section 174A of the Internal Revenue Code as part of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act. The statute provides a deduction for domestic software research and development expenditure.

The language is broad: "any amounts paid or incurred in connection with the development of software." Not reasonable amounts. Not amounts approved by a CPA. Any amounts. The deduction applies to domestic R&D — software developed in the United States.

Congress writes tax law to incentivize specific economic activity. Section 174A is designed to encourage software development by making the cost of that development deductible. The deduction is available to anyone who develops software domestically — sole developers, small teams, large organizations.

The statute is new. There is limited case law, limited regulatory guidance, and limited infrastructure for claiming the deduction. The law exists. The machinery for proving compliance is still being built.

The deduction is straightforward.
The documentation is not.

174A created an engineering problem

The Engineering Problem

The Challenge

Proving what you built,
when you built it, and why.

To claim a deduction under 174A, you need contemporaneous records — documentation created at the time of the work, not reconstructed at tax time. What was developed, when, by whom, and for how long. That is not a legal problem. It is an engineering problem.

The requirements map directly to infrastructure: timestamped records, encrypted storage, version history, structured logs. These are the same primitives that underlie any serious document system. The question is whether they exist in a form that is organized, verifiable, and exportable.

For most developers, they do not. Chat logs are scattered. Git commits are unstructured for documentation purposes. Development decisions live in memory, not in records. Hours are untracked. By the time tax season arrives, the evidence either does not exist or cannot be assembled into anything useful.

This is the same class of problem that appears across regulatory and legal text: the tax code itself is a semi-structured dataset — 106,109 pages published in the Federal Register in 2024 alone. Regulatory text is non-stationary, shifting across administrations. It is not a blob, not a database. Navigating it at scale requires computational methods. So does generating the documentation it demands.

The record has to exist from the beginning.
Not after the fact.

Contemporaneous documentation, by design.

Timestamped Transcripts

Every development session is recorded in .claude/Transcripts/ with full timestamps. Who said what, when, and why. Design decisions, technical direction, rejected approaches — all captured as they happen. Not reconstructed. Contemporaneous.

02

Encrypted Records

Development records are stored as structured TimeEntry objects — each with a timestamp, duration, author, source file reference, and raw metadata. Records are sealed in an encrypted container. AES-GCM encryption on every EditTransaction before it touches disk.

AtlasRecordView
03

Version Snapshots

Every keystroke produces a timestamped EditTransaction — a structured record of what changed, when, and in what context. Git integration provides version history. Auto-save commits create an unbroken chain of snapshots that document the entire development timeline.

EditTransaction
04

Time Tracking

The TimeEntryView extracts and organizes metadata from every artifact in your development record — file creation dates, modification timestamps, device identifiers, GPS data. Duration is calculated from log spans. Each entry maps to structured fields for qualified research expenditure.

Privacy by Architecture

Your records stay on your machine.

Redliner processes everything on-device. No server. No API calls. No data leaves your machine. Edit transactions are encrypted with AES-GCM before they touch disk. The application runs in Apple's sandbox with process-level isolation via XPC services. The Secure Enclave provides hardware-backed key storage. Your development records — transcripts, timesheets, logs, version history — live in your repository, under your control.
