The Dataset

One of the most interesting
semi-structured datasets on earth.

Congress.gov is the official source for U.S. federal legislative information, maintained by the Library of Congress. It contains every bill and resolution introduced in Congress, every amendment offered, every vote cast, every word spoken on the floor.

The 118th Congress (2023-2024) saw over 19,000 bills and resolutions introduced in the House alone. The Congressional Record documents every floor statement verbatim. Committee reports explain legislative intent. Conference reports reconcile differences between chambers. Treaty documents record the Senate's advice and consent.

This data exists in multiple representations: USLM XML with deep structural markup, HTML renderings for web display, plain text for search, PDF for official publication. Same content, different serializations. A single bill might amend dozens of sections across multiple titles of the United States Code, each amendment carrying its own effective date, sunset clause, and cross-reference network.

All of this is freely available. It belongs to the American people. It is, by constitutional design, public information. The question is not access. The question is what you do with it once you have it.

Not a database. Not a blob. Something in between that requires real computational methods.

Semi-structured data is the hardest kind to parse correctly.

Engineering
01

Legislative Graph

Bills amend statutes. Statutes are codified into titles. Titles cross-reference other titles. Amendments modify specific sections of existing law. Conference reports reconcile divergent versions. This is a directed acyclic graph -- not a flat document. The parser models it as one: each SECBlock carries typed edges to the nodes it modifies, with heading1, heading2, definition, and listItem types preserving the hierarchy.

Directed Acyclic Graph
02

Semi-Structured Parsing

Legislative markup varies: XML (USLM format), HTML, plain text, PDF. The parser infrastructure handles all of them. SECHTMLParser uses compiled regex to extract paragraph-level blocks from HTML, classifying each by its attribute soup -- class="H1" becomes .heading1, bullet entities become .listItem. The HTMLVisitor walks the tree node by node, emitting structured output. stripTags(from:) strips internal markup to clean text.

Multi-Format Parser
03

On-Device Classification

The NaturalLanguage framework provides tokenization, entity recognition, and sentiment analysis -- all on-device, all without a network call. AtlasMLManager records semantic tagging events as JSONL training data: each entry captures a label, a cleaned text via NLTokenizer(unit: .word), and the raw context. AtlasTagger extracts tagged chunks from NSTextStorage by enumerating the custom .atlasSemanticTag attribute.

Mathematical Foundations
The math
behind
the parsing.
Topology
Legislative structure forms directed acyclic graphs. Bills amend statutes, which are codified into titles, which cross-reference other titles. Understanding connectivity, cycles, and reachability is a topological problem.
Spectral Methods
Eigendecomposition of bill similarity matrices enables clustering across sessions. Bills that amend the same statute, share sponsors, or use similar language cluster in spectral space even when introduced years apart.
Manifolds
Each amendment is a small perturbation in document space. Successive drafts of a bill trace a smooth curve on a manifold of legislative texts, and the geometry of that manifold encodes the drafting process itself.
Fisher Information
What do co-sponsorship patterns actually tell you about bill content? Fisher information quantifies the information that observable variables (sponsors, committees, vote counts) carry about latent legislative dimensions.
Non-Stationary Time Series
Legislative language evolves. "Interstate commerce" means something different in 1935 and 2025. Drafting conventions shift across administrations. The statistical properties of the text stream change with time -- stationarity cannot be assumed.
Convergence
Conference committees are optimization in action. House and Senate versions of a bill are two points in document space; the conference report is the result of a constrained convergence process where political and procedural constraints define the feasible set.
